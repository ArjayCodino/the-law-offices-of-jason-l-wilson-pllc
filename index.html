What are the Requirements?

To receive disability benefits, you must have a medical condition that prevents you from working and meet the SSA's list of impairments. If not listed, you must demonstrate the severity of your condition. You must also have worked long enough to qualify. A skilled attorney can assist with the claims process and medical evidence collection.

SSI vs SSDI

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) and Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) are two types of disability programs that provide financial assistance to individuals with disabilities. While SSI is based on financial need, SSDI is based on work history and contributions to the Social Security system, including social security taxes paid by workers. An applicant needs a social security number and enough years' worth of social security taxes paid to be eligible for SSDI benefits. In contrast, the eligibility for SSI depends on medical eligibility along with an individual's income and resources. With the help of an experienced attorney, a claimant can navigate the application process for both programs easily.



The Disability Claim Process

  • Initial Application:The process begins by filing an initial application with the relevant government agency, such as the Social Security Administration (SSA) in the United States. You'll need to provide detailed information about your medical condition, work history, and other relevant details.
  • Medical Documentation:To support your claim, you'll need to gather and submit medical records, doctor's statements, test results, and any other evidence that demonstrates the severity and impact of your disability. This documentation helps establish the existence and extent of your disabling condition.
  • Disability Determination:TAfter receiving your application and supporting documents, the government agency will review your case. They will assess your medical evidence, work history, and other factors to determine if you meet their criteria for disability. This evaluation typically involves medical professionals and disability examiners.
  • Denial or Approval:Based on the evaluation, your claim may be approved, denied, or require additional information. If approved, you will start receiving disability benefits. If denied, you can appeal the decision and request a reconsideration.
  • Appeals and Hearings:You can appeal the decision if your claim is initially denied. This usually involves a series of stages, including a reconsideration request, administrative hearings, and, in some cases, a review by an appeals council. You can submit additional evidence during the appeals process and present your case before an administrative law judge.
